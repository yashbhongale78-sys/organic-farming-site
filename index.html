About Organic Farming

Organic farming uses natural inputs and ecological processes to build healthy soil, conserve water, and reduce chemical use. It supports biodiversity and produces nutritious food.

Our site focuses on practical guides you can use at home, community farming, and smallholdings. Content includes step-by-step tutorials, seasonal calendars, and vetted external resources.

Core Practices

  • Crop rotation: Rotate crops to improve soil health and control pests.
  • Composting: Recycle organic waste into nutrient-rich compost.
  • Cover crops: Prevent erosion and fix nitrogen in the soil.
  • Natural pest control: Use beneficial insects and companion planting.
  • Mulching: Reduce water loss and suppress weeds.
  • Soil testing: Test pH and nutrients before major amendments.

Soil: Foundation of Organic Farming

Soil is a living ecosystem. Healthy soil supports plant growth, stores water, cycles nutrients, and suppresses disease.

Key soil characteristics

  • Structure: Aggregation and porosity determine root penetration and drainage.
  • Organic matter: Aim for steady increases — improves water retention, nutrient exchange and microbial life.
  • pH & nutrients: Most vegetables prefer pH 6.0–7.0; test for N, P, K, Ca, Mg and micronutrients.
  • Biology: Earthworms, bacteria, fungi and microarthropods drive nutrient cycling — protect them by avoiding harsh chemicals.

Practical soil tests and actions

  • Start with a basic soil test from your extension service. Interpret results before adding lime or fertilizer.
  • Add compost regularly (1–3 cm per year), use cover crops to capture nutrients and build organic matter.
  • Minimize tillage where feasible to preserve fungal networks and soil structure.

Amendments & Composting (Detailed)

Compost best practices

  • Balance greens (nitrogen: kitchen scraps, fresh greens) and browns (carbon: straw, dry leaves). Target ~2:1 browns:greens by volume.
  • Maintain moisture like a wrung-out sponge; turn periodically to aerate and speed decomposition.
  • Hot composting (55–65°C) kills many weed seeds and pathogens; smaller piles will decompose slower but still produce good compost.

Common amendments

  • Well-rotted manure: Adds nutrients and organic matter, apply carefully to avoid burning plants.
  • Bone meal, rock phosphate: Slow-release P sources for low-phosphorus soils.
  • Lime or sulfur: Adjust pH only after testing; apply gradually and retest.
  • Biochar: Improves retention of nutrients and water when charged with compost.

Pest & Disease Management (IPM)

Use integrated pest management (IPM): prevention first, monitoring, then targeted interventions.

Prevention

  • Crop rotation, resistant varieties, proper spacing and good sanitation reduce pressure.
  • Encourage beneficial insects with habitat plants and avoid broad-spectrum pesticides.

Monitoring & thresholds

  • Inspect plants weekly. Use sticky traps and pheromone traps where appropriate.
  • Intervene only when pest levels exceed economic or aesthetic thresholds.

Control options

  • Physical: hand-picking, barriers, row covers.
  • Biological: release or attract predators/parasitoids (ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps).
  • Botanical and organic-approved products: neem oil, insecticidal soaps, Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) for caterpillars.

How to Build a Compost Bin

Choose a shady spot, mix greens (kitchen scraps) and browns (dry leaves), keep moist and turn weekly. Aim for a balance of roughly 2 parts browns to 1 part greens.

Seasonal Tips

Quick month-by-month actions for a temperate climate (adjust for your zone):

  • Spring: Prep beds, sow cool-season crops, start seedlings indoors.
  • Summer: Mulch, irrigate early morning, watch for pests and mildew.
  • Autumn: Plant cover crops, collect seeds, add compost.
  • Winter: Plan crop rotations, repair tools, order seeds.

Organic Certification (Overview)

Certification verifies compliance with organic standards for marketing as "organic". Requirements differ by country/region but commonly include:

  • Transition period (commonly 2–3 years) with no prohibited synthetic inputs.
  • Record keeping for inputs, seed sources, sales and field history.
  • Written organic system plan detailing practices and inputs.
  • Annual inspection by an accredited certifier.

Many small producers sell direct or use specific labels for "raised organically" — check local certifier rules for details and costs.

Economics & Marketing

Planning & budgeting

  • Estimate startup costs (beds, tools, seeds, soil amendments) and recurring costs (labor, compost, irrigation).
  • Small-scale organic operations often rely on direct-to-consumer sales (farmers markets, CSAs, on-farm sales) to capture higher margins.

Value-added & diversification

  • Consider processed goods, preserved produce, workshops, or agritourism to diversify income.

Grants & support

  • Search local agricultural extension programs, grants, and low-interest loans for beginning farmers and sustainable projects.

FAQ

Do I need a large plot to start organic farming?

No — many organic techniques work well for small plots, containers, and backyards.

How do I start composting?

Combine greens (kitchen scraps) and browns (dry leaves), maintain moisture and occasional turning.

How long before my soil improves?

You will see changes in soil biology and tilth within a season, but significant organic matter increases may take several years of consistent inputs and cover cropping.
